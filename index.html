Tre Flip

the whole body guide / goofy / crooked setup

Your scoop is clean. Your flick is there. But your front foot disappears into space and never comes back.

That's not a foot problem. That's a body problem. When your shoulders rotate or your eyes chase the board, your front foot follows them away from the deck. Fix the body, and the feet fix themselves.

This guide covers three truths about your upper body, then shows you exactly what your front foot does when everything above it is right. No seven-phase breakdowns. No over-thinking. Just what your body needs to know.

Truth 1
1

Your Shoulders Aim Your Feet

Your shoulders are the steering wheel. Wherever they point at takeoff is where your feet will land. If your shoulders open even 15 degrees during the scoop, your front foot follows that rotation out into space. It's not a foot problem — it's your shoulders leaving the grid.

The fix is dead simple: lock your shoulders square to your direction of travel. They don't move. Not before, not during, not after. Your back ankle does the scoop. Your hips stay level. Your shoulders stay exactly where they started. Think of your belt buckle — it should face the same direction when you land as when you popped.

"Belt buckle forward. Shoulders are concrete."

Truth 2
2

Your Eyes Pick the Landing

Your feet go where your eyes go. If you track the board spinning beneath you with hard focus, your body chases it. If you look away in fear, your feet bail. Neither works.

Soft focus, straight down. Don't track the board's corkscrew rotation — you can't follow it anyway. Instead, relax your eyes and look at the area where the board left from. Your peripheral vision processes motion faster than your central vision. It will catch the flash of grip tape reappearing. That flash is your "now" signal.

Your eyes should be aimed at a point between your feet — roughly where the front bolts are. Not at the tail, not at the nose, not at the ground six feet away. Right between your feet. Your peripheral vision handles everything else.

side view — soft focus down at the bolt zone, peripheral vision catches the grip flash

"Eyes down. Soft. Let the board come to you."

Truth 3
3

Your Hips Are the Anchor

Think of your body as a tree. Shoulders are the canopy — locked, wide, still. Hips are the trunk — centered, level, calm. Everything below the hips has freedom: your back ankle scoops, your front ankle flicks, your knees pull up. But the hips don't move.

The scoop generates a strong backside rotational pull on your body. Your hips want to follow it. Don't let them. The twist stores in your core and dissipates. Your hips stay level. If you land with your body rotated 45 degrees, it's not a foot problem or a scoop problem — it's your hips following the scoop.

"Tree trunk. Level. The storm is below you."

The Front Foot

When Everything Above Is Right, This Is All Your Front Foot Does

If your shoulders are locked, your eyes are soft, and your hips are still — your front foot only has to do three simple things in order. No compartmentalization. Just a natural sequence that flows from the right body position.

1. Tap at 12:30

Your front foot rolls off the toe-side edge near the nose. Not a kick — a tap. Your scoop is already doing 60–70% of the flip work. The front foot just adds the last bit of flip energy. Exit angle is 12:30 on a clock face — straight forward with a tiny bit of toe-side. Any more lateral than that and you get a double flip.

2. Curl to Your Knee

This is the move you're missing. The instant your foot leaves the board, it curls UP — not out. Toes point down. Your foot pulls back toward your front knee. It doesn't travel away from the board. It hovers directly above where the nose is going to be. Think of it like closing a door — it swings shut, not open.

The distance between your hovering foot and the board should be inches, not feet. If your foot is a foot away from the deck, you curled too late or your shoulders pulled it out.

3. See Grip, Stomp

Your peripheral vision catches the flash of grip tape completing the rotation. That's your "now" signal. Drop your front foot straight down. Don't reach for the board — it's right there beneath your hovering foot. Stomp it. Your back foot follows immediately. Pinch both feet — front presses forward, back presses backward — to kill any residual spin.

Your soft front bushings (Bones 91a) absorb any slight off-center landing. The setup forgives you. Commit to the stomp.

The Confident Catch

Both Feet. No Fear. No Slip.

The reason you're afraid to stomp is that your front foot is too far away when the board comes around. When your front foot is hovering inches above the nose, there's nothing to be afraid of. The board is right there. You just drop onto it.

The catch is both feet simultaneously. Front foot drops onto the front bolts. Back foot meets the back bolts at the same time or a split second after. The moment both feet touch grip, pinch — front foot presses toward the nose, back foot presses toward the tail. This grind pinch kills any residual spin energy so the board doesn't keep rotating under you. That's what prevents the slip-out.

Land over your bolts, not in the middle of the board. Bolts are where the trucks are — that's structural support. Landing in the middle flexes the deck and creates instability. Knees absorb. Landing should be quiet. If it's loud, you're stomping from too high or skipping the carry phase.

"Pinch. Both feet press outward from center. Board stops spinning. You're on."

Drills

Four Drills to Wire It In

Do these in order before every tre flip session. They build the muscle memory for the curl-hover-stomp sequence without the pressure of landing a full trick.

1 Stationary Ankle Roll 20 reps

Stand on the board. Front foot in flick position. Without popping, roll your ankle through the flick motion — toe drags off the edge at 12:30. Focus on what happens after the roll: your foot curls up, toes point down, foot pulls toward your knee. Practice the curl, not the flick.

2 Hover Hold 10 reps × 3 sec

Stand on one foot (back foot on ground). Simulate the full flick motion with your front foot. After the "flick," hold the hover position for 3 seconds — toes pointed down, foot at knee height, directly above where the board would be. Build the muscle memory for that specific position.

3 The Stamp 10 reps

Pop a real tre flip. Don't try to land it. Your only goal is to get your front foot to make contact with the grip tape. Even if you step off with your back foot, even if you bail — the front foot must touch the board. Success = front foot touched grip. That's it.

4 One-Foot Catch 10 reps

Pop a regular kickflip. Catch it with only your front foot — back foot stays off the board or on the ground. This forces your front foot to find the deck and teaches it that the board is always right there. Builds catch confidence independent of the tre flip.

The Shift of Knowing

Tre flips have one of the most dramatic shifts in skateboarding. Dozens of failed attempts, then suddenly everything aligns and you land one clean. When it clicks, stop thinking. Don't analyze what changed. Land 10 more immediately. The first 20 reps after the shift are worth more than 100 before it.
